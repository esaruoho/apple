# Steve Jobs's Final Message: Autobiography of a Yogi

Research compiled 2026-03-30.

---

## 1. The Small Brown Box

**Confirmed.** At Steve Jobs's memorial service, held at Stanford University on October 16, 2011, each attendee received a small brown box as they departed. Jobs had planned every detail of the service himself. The box contained a copy of *Autobiography of a Yogi* (1946) by Paramahansa Yogananda.

One attendee wrote: "When I first discovered what was inside the small, brown box, I was speechless. It was a book. Not about technology or how to start a company, but a book by an Indian guru, Paramahansa Yogananda."

The Self-Realization Fellowship reportedly received a call: "We'd like to place an order for 500 copies of Autobiography of a Yogi. Do you have that many?" (Nabha Cosley account)

---

## 2. Marc Benioff's Account

**Confirmed.** Marc Benioff (CEO of Salesforce) told this story publicly, most notably at TechCrunch Disrupt in September 2013. His key quotes:

On opening the box:
> "I said, 'This is gonna be good,' because I knew it was the last thing Jobs wanted us to all think about. I waited until I got to my car and I opened the box and... it was a copy of Yogananda's book."

On the message:
> "Yogananda was a Hindu guru who had this... book on self-realization. And that was the message -- 'Actualize yourself.' That was Yogananda's message."

On understanding Jobs through the book:
> "If you want to understand Steve Jobs, it's a good idea to [read 'Autobiography of a Yogi'] because... it gives a tremendous insight into who he was, but also why he was successful."

> "He was not afraid to take that key journey. And that is for entrepreneurs and for people who want to be successful in our industry, a message that we need to embrace."

On Jobs as a spiritual person:
> "I look at Steve as a very spiritual person... I remember his thought that we need to all be working on actualizing [ourselves]."

---

## 3. Walter Isaacson's Biography Confirmation

**Confirmed.** Walter Isaacson's authorized biography *Steve Jobs* (2011) states:

> "[He] first read the book as a teenager, then reread it in India and had read it once a year ever since."

Additionally, it was reported that *Autobiography of a Yogi* was the **only book** Steve Jobs had downloaded on his iPad before he left for his final Hawaiian vacation.

---

## 4. The Benioff "Intuition" and "Inside Out" Quote

**Confirmed.** Benioff said:

> "If you look back at the history of Steve -- he went to India to... the ashram of his maharishi and he had this incredible realization -- that... his intuition was his greatest gift and... he needed to look at the world from the inside out."

A slightly condensed version from another source:
> "That was his message. Actualize yourself. He had this incredible realization that his intuition was his greatest gift, and he needed to look at the world from inside out."

---

## 5. Jobs's Deliberate Final Message

Multiple details confirm this was a deliberate, planned act -- not an afterthought:

- **Jobs planned the entire memorial service himself**, including every detail, despite his deteriorating health in his final weeks.
- **500 copies were ordered** from the Self-Realization Fellowship in advance.
- **It was the only book on his iPad** -- not one of many, the only one.
- **Annual re-reading** -- he had read it every year since his teens/India trip, making it arguably the most important text in his life.
- **Benioff's interpretation**: "I knew that this was a decision he made, and whatever it was, it was the last thing he wanted us all to think about."

Jobs himself spoke about what the book's teachings meant to him:

> "I began to realize that an intuitive understanding and consciousness was more significant than abstract thinking and intellectual, logical analysis."

> "That's when your intuition starts to blossom and you start to see things more clearly and be in the present more."

The message was unmistakable: the man who built the world's most valuable technology company wanted his final word to be about self-realization, not technology. He chose a book about meditation, inner awareness, and spiritual development as his parting gift to 500 of the most influential people in Silicon Valley.
