# Roger Rosner: The Architect of Apple Keynote

*Research compiled 2026-03-30*

## Summary

Roger Rock Rosner is the person who turned Steve Jobs's presentation obsession into software — twice. He built Concurrence for NeXTSTEP in 1990, which Jobs used to replace glass slides forever. A decade later, he built Keynote at Apple, which Jobs used for every Macworld keynote from 2003 until his death. As of 2026, Rosner remains VP of Applications at Apple, a role he has held since 2013 — one of the longest-tenured VPs at the company. He has never given a public interview about building Keynote or working with Jobs.

---

## 1. Is He Still Alive? What Is He Doing Now?

**Yes, alive and still at Apple.** As of the latest available data (2024-2025), Roger Rosner holds the title **Vice President of Applications** at Apple Inc. He has been at Apple since March 2001 — over 25 years.

His current portfolio spans far beyond iWork:
- **Productivity apps**: Pages, Numbers, Keynote
- **Creative apps**: GarageBand, iMovie, Final Cut Pro, Clips
- **Media apps**: Apple News, Apple Books
- He has progressively absorbed new app categories over the past decade

He was featured prominently in the **Harvard Business Review** (November 2020) article "How Apple Is Organized for Innovation" by Apple University president Joel Podolny, where his leadership approach was used as a case study for Apple's "discretionary leadership" model.

## 2. Interviews and Media Appearances

**The striking finding: there are essentially NO interviews with Roger Rosner.** No podcast appearances, no conference talks, no magazine profiles, no oral histories. For someone who has been at Apple for 25 years and built one of Jobs's most personal tools, he is remarkably invisible.

### What exists:

**On-stage Apple events (not interviews, but public appearances):**
- **WWDC 2011**: Demoed iCloud "Documents in the Cloud" on stage alongside Steve Jobs. This was Jobs's second-to-last WWDC and one of Rosner's first widely-viewed public appearances.
- **January 2012**: Demoed **iBooks Author** at Apple's education event in New York — the digital textbook initiative he led.
- **WWDC 2013**: Unveiled **iWork for iCloud** (Keynote, Pages, Numbers running in web browsers).
- **March 25, 2019**: Presented **Apple News+** at the Steve Jobs Theater, Apple Park. This is his most prominent solo stage appearance. Tim Cook introduced him. He said: *"We wanted to create the best magazine reading experience ever for a mobile device."*

**Harvard Business Review profile (November 2020):**
"How Apple Is Organized for Innovation" by Joel M. Podolny (Dean, Apple University) and Morten T. Hansen (Faculty, Apple University). HBR Reprint R2006F. PDF stored at `analysis/HBR-how-apple-is-organized-for-innovation-2020.pdf`.

Rosner is the central case study. Key details:

**The Discretionary Leadership model (Rosner's four boxes):**

| | Highly involved in details | Not highly involved |
|---|---|---|
| **High expertise** | **OWNING (40%)**: Parts of News, UI design, Software architecture | **TEACHING (15%)**: Keynote, Pages, Numbers |
| **Low expertise** | **LEARNING (30%)**: Parts of News, Voice memos, Weather | **DELEGATING (15%)**: iMovie, Final Cut Pro, GarageBand |

**Keynote is in the TEACHING box** — Rosner moved it there from OWNING. He's so deep in Keynote that he now teaches others how to build software to Apple's norms through it.

**On Rosner specifically (p.7):**
> "One current example is Roger Rosner, who heads Apple's software application business, which includes work-productivity apps such as Pages (word processing), Numbers (spreadsheets), and Keynote (presentations) along with GarageBand (music composition), iMovie (movie editing), and News (an app providing news content). Rosner, who studied electrical engineering at Carnegie Mellon, joined Apple in 2001 as a senior engineering manager and rose to become the director of iWork applications, the vice president of productivity apps, and since 2013 the VP of applications. With his deep expertise gained from previous experience as the director of engineering at several smaller software companies, Rosner exemplifies an expert leading experts."

**On teaching vs. owning (p.11):**
> "He has moved some things from his *owning* box — including traditional productivity apps such as Keynote and Pages — into his *teaching* box. Now he guides and gives feedback to other team members so that they can develop software applications according to Apple's norms. Being a teacher doesn't mean that Rosner gives instruction at a whiteboard; rather, he offers strong, often passionate critiques of his team's work."

**On learning (Apple News, p.11):**
> "Six years ago he was given responsibility for the engineering and design of News. Consequently, he had to learn about publishing news content via an app — to understand news publications, digital advertising, machine learning to personalize news content, architecting for privacy, and how to incentivize publishers... Over six years of intense learning, Rosner has mastered some of these areas, which are now in his owning box."

**On the learning box mindset (p.11):**
> "As long as a particular activity remains in the learning box, leaders must adopt a beginner's mindset, questioning subordinates in a way that suggests they don't already know the answer (because they don't). This differs starkly from the way leaders question subordinates about activities in the owning and teaching boxes."

**Steve Jobs on experts vs. managers (1984 interview, quoted p.7):**
> "We went through that stage in Apple where we went out and thought, *Oh, we're gonna be a big company, let's hire professional management.* We went out and hired a bunch of professional management. It didn't work at all....They knew how to manage, but they didn't know how to *do* anything. If you're a great person, why do you want to work for somebody you can't learn anything from? And you know what's interesting? You know who the best managers are? They are the great individual contributors who never, ever want to be a manager but decide they have to be...because no one else is going to...do as good a job."

**Apple's fundamental belief (p.5):**
> "Its fundamental belief is that those with the most expertise and experience in a domain should have decision rights for that domain."

**"Accountability without control" (p.9):**
> "At Apple that's known as *accountability without control*: You're accountable for making the project succeed even though you don't control all the other teams."

**The squircle detail (p.9):**
Apple uses continuous curves ("squircles") instead of standard rounded rectangles for product corners — requires more complex math and tighter manufacturing tolerances, but produces softer light highlights. "This deep immersion in detail isn't just a concern that is pushed down to lower-level people; it is central at the leadership level."

**Three leadership characteristics (p.6):**
1. Deep expertise
2. Immersion in the details
3. Willingness to collaboratively debate

**No interviews exist** about:
- Building Concurrence
- Building Keynote
- Working with Steve Jobs
- His presentation design philosophy
- The Lighthouse Design years
- The transition from NeXTSTEP to Mac OS X

This is a significant gap in the historical record.

## 5. Full Career Arc

### Education
- **Carnegie Mellon University** — B.S. in Electrical Engineering and B.S. in Computer Engineering, 1987

### Lighthouse Design Ltd. (1989-1996)
- **Co-founded August 1989** in Bethesda, Maryland, with four others: Alan Chung, Jonathan Schwartz, Kevin Steele, and Brian Skinner
- Later relocated to San Mateo, California
- **Project leader and principal architect of Concurrence** — the presentation app for NeXTSTEP
- Also worked on other Lighthouse products (Diagram!, Quantrix, etc.)
- **Left Lighthouse "soon after [Concurrence] shipped"** — he did not stay through the Sun acquisition
- Lighthouse built a respected NeXTSTEP office suite: Concurrence (presentations), Quantrix (spreadsheets), Diagram! (vector graphics), ParaSheet, TaskMaster, WetPaint, LightPlan

### Sun Microsystems acquisition of Lighthouse (1996)
- Sun bought Lighthouse for **$22 million** in mid-1996
- Rosner was already gone by this point
- Jonathan Schwartz rose through Sun to become CEO in 2006
- The Lighthouse apps were never ported to Java or released for Mac OS X
- Schwartz said Sun was "not really trying to promote Objective-C anymore"
- The code went dormant — binaries were made available on a memorial website for NeXT/OpenStep users

### Gap period (~1996-2000)
- What Rosner did between leaving Lighthouse and founding Blue Fish Labs is unclear from public records

### Blue Fish Labs (2000-2001)
- **Co-founded July 2000** — Rosner served as CEO
- Headquartered in **Munhall, Pennsylvania** (near Pittsburgh, his Carnegie Mellon roots)
- Described as an "internet software and services company" providing "software products and engineering services for e-business Web sites and Internet applications"
- **Acquired by Apple on April 1, 2001** for an undisclosed amount
- This was an acqui-hire — Apple wanted Rosner

### Apple (2001-present)
- **2001**: Joined as **Senior Engineering Manager** (via Blue Fish Labs acquisition)
- **~2003**: Led development of **Keynote 1.0**, announced by Steve Jobs at Macworld Expo, January 7, 2003. Sold for $99. Jobs used it for every keynote thereafter.
- **2004**: Promoted to **Director of iWork** (Pages, Numbers, Keynote)
- **2006**: Promoted to **VP of Productivity Applications**
- **2011**: Demoed iCloud Documents in the Cloud alongside Jobs at WWDC (Jobs's penultimate WWDC)
- **2012**: Led the **iBooks Author** / digital textbook initiative — personally demoed it at Apple's NYC education event
- **September 2013**: Promoted to **VP of Applications** — expanded portfolio beyond iWork
- **2019**: Presented **Apple News+** at Steve Jobs Theater (March 25, 2019)
- **2020**: Featured in HBR as case study for Apple's discretionary leadership model
- **2024-present**: Still listed as VP of Applications across all public sources

### What he now oversees (as of ~2020 HBR article):
Over the past decade he has progressively absorbed: Apple News, Clips (video editing), Apple Books, Final Cut Pro (advanced video editing), in addition to the original iWork suite and GarageBand/iMovie.

## 6. The Concurrence-to-Keynote Pipeline

This is the most important story, and it has never been told in Rosner's own words.

### Concurrence (1990-1992)
- Built by Rosner at Lighthouse Design for NeXTSTEP
- Ran on NeXT hardware, later on any NeXTSTEP/OpenStep system
- **Steve Jobs used it for the first time to prepare his 1992 NeXTWORLD Expo keynote**
- Before Concurrence, Jobs used **glass slides** — expensive, inflexible, requiring weeks of lead time
- Concurrence let Jobs refine slides "literally until the moment he took the stage"
- Jobs's 1992 keynote: 73 slides, 26MB total, used NeXTstation Turbo Color
- Technical details from Jobs's actual 1992 setup:
  - Tekton font (chosen for projector readability)
  - 1120 x 832 pixel page dimensions (to fill the monitor)
  - "Chalkboard-green" background (suggested by marketing VP Mike Slade, replacing original dark blue)
  - Used Slide View rather than Outline View for immediate visual feedback
  - Built progressive reveals by duplicating slides with successive bullet points
  - Overlaid text as TIFF images with transparency over photos
  - Drew colored rectangles behind table rows for emphasis
- Jobs called it "the best slide show he has ever done, thanks to Concurrence"

### The gap (1996-2001)
- Lighthouse sold to Sun. Code goes dormant.
- Jobs returns to Apple (1997). He is still using Concurrence — reportedly on an IBM ThinkPad running OpenStep at Macworld Expo.
- Jobs "spoke highly of Lighthouse Design during the 1997 WWDC video"
- No modern presentation tool meets his standards — PowerPoint is despised

### Keynote 1.0 (2003)
- Apple acquires Blue Fish Labs (and Rosner) in April 2001
- By 2003, Rosner has built Keynote — the spiritual successor to Concurrence
- "Early versions of Keynote are observed to have closely resembled Concurrence"
- Keynote uses Quartz and OpenGL for anti-aliased text, transparency, dynamic shadows, cinematic transitions
- Sold for $99. Jobs announces it at Macworld Expo January 7, 2003.
- Jobs uses it for every keynote thereafter until his death in 2011

### The Schwartz confrontation
- When Jobs threatened to sue Sun over Project Looking Glass resembling Mac OS X
- Sun CEO Jonathan Schwartz (Lighthouse co-founder) countered: "Keynote looks identical to Concurrence — do you own that IP?"
- Jobs went silent. Neither lawsuit was filed.
- This confirms at the highest level that the lineage from Concurrence to Keynote was known and acknowledged

## 7. Why This Matters

Roger Rosner is the person who gave Steve Jobs his visual voice. Before Concurrence, Jobs used glass slides. After Concurrence, Jobs had a medium that matched his obsessive, last-minute, detail-oriented presentation style. Then Rosner built Keynote, which became the canvas for every iconic Apple product reveal from 2003 onward — the iPod nano pulled from the jeans pocket, the iPhone "one more thing," the MacBook Air from the manila envelope.

And yet Rosner has never spoken publicly about any of it. No oral history, no memoir, no retrospective interview. He is perhaps the most consequential silent figure in Apple's history — a 25-year VP who built Jobs's most intimate creative tool and never said a word about it.

---

## 8. Unanswered Questions

1. **What happened between ~1993 and 2000?** Rosner left Lighthouse "soon after Concurrence shipped" but didn't found Blue Fish Labs until 2000. Seven years unaccounted for.
2. **Did Jobs personally recruit Rosner to Apple?** The Blue Fish Labs acquisition in 2001 looks like a pure acqui-hire. Was Jobs behind it?
3. **What was Blue Fish Labs actually building?** It's described vaguely as "internet software and services." Was it a pretext to get Rosner into Apple?
4. **What does Rosner think about Keynote's evolution?** The app he built for Jobs's glass-slide-to-digital transition is now a commodity tool. How does he feel about that?
5. **Will he ever speak?** At 61 (roughly, based on 1987 graduation), he is approaching retirement age. An oral history with the Computer History Museum or similar institution would be invaluable.
